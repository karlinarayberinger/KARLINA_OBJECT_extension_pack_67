I was hesitant to write and publish this note because I have already published a note somewhere in KARBYTES (likely the summer of 2024 while I was camping near some apparently abandonned warehouse in Sunnyvale, California as part of my intensive tour of the Silicon Valley region and baylands recreational areas especially) about how I voluntarily killed a female black widow spider which was near my camping spot. Also, I wanted to minimize how much I update KARBYTES during the summer months of 2026 because this period of time is supposed to be the first official major hiatus of KARBYTES. Of course, I am the sole rule maker, sole rule enforcer, and sole rule editor of KARBYTES as its sole original proprietor and author. Therefore, I can make modest exceptions to my rules at times. For instance, I can include this little anecdote about how I unexpectedly was summoned to go to battle with a potentially lethally poisonous black widow spider in my room last night.

When I first noticed the black widow spider in my room, I decided to leave it alone since it seemed difficult to catch the spider without it biting me. I was afraid of making the spider feel threatened to the extent that it would want to approach me and bite me in vengence. Well, it seemed that it was eyeing me for a long time after sunset and after I decided to draw the curtains shut over the open sliding glass door while the screen door was shut and the spider was in the top right corner of that screen door. About an hour later, I saw the spider looking down at me on the other side of the curtains and decided to try entertaining it by trying to put on the best performance of what a miniature god behind the scenes might look like (because I imagine humans look godlike compared to spiders from spiders’ points of view). Several hours after that I noticed the spider wasn’t anywhere I could see it. It seemed to not be wary of the “artificial” light from my light-emitting-diode color-changing lamp but, as soon as sunlight reached the room, the spider went into hiding. I figured the spider’s dissappearance was just a normal part of its nocturnal circadian rhythms. When it became night again, that’s when the spider became so bold as to start building a web around where I was lying prone on the floor. I noticed it descending from the bed towards the floor within inches of my left forearm and face. Instantly I jumped up and backwards and yelled in fright and disgust. The spider seemed to be trying to approach me like it wanted to fight me. I decided that I had to defend myself and that killing the spider was not exactly unethical for me to do in that instance by my reasoning.

The spider was more difficult to kill than the one I mentioned killing a few years ago outside because the ground was hard cement (which meant that the shoe I whacked the spider with cleanly smashed the spider in one blow). The carpet in my room prevented the spider from getting squished right away (and what I was doing was dropping large gallon-sized water bottles at it). The spider escaped at least two times and seemed no less emboldened to approach me. I decided I needed to throw the water jugs down with all my might to crush the spider’s body. That did it! I threw the jug with maximum force three or four times to make sure the spider was dead. It left a reddish purple spot on the rug. I scooped up the remains with a piece of cardboard into a small trash can and then ejected its contents outside the room. I wondered it the venom from the spider would have any effect on my body if I inhaled it or came into contact with that puddle. So far, I seem to be faring just fine.

The purpose of this anecdote is to showcase how I cannot always adhere to my nonviolent ideals. Sometimes I feel compelled to commit violence (and even murder) in defense of myself or other beings I feel compelled to defend.
